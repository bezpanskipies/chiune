Chiune Sugihara

The man who saved Jews

Here's a time line of Chiune Sugihara's life:

  • 1900 – Chiune Sugihara was born on January 1 in Yaotsu, Gifu Prefecture, Japan, into a samurai-descended family.
  • 1918–1924, – Studied foreign languages and international relations, becoming fluent in Russian, German, and English; later joined the Japanese Foreign Ministry.
  • 1939 – Assigned as Vice-Consul to the Japanese Consulate in Kaunas, Lithuania, just as World War II was beginning.
  • July 1940 – Began issuing transit visas against direct orders from Tokyo, helping Jewish refugees escape Nazi-occupied Europe via Japan.
  • August 1940 – Continued writing visas for long hours each day, even tossing signed documents from the train window as he was forced to leave Lithuania.
  • 1941–1946 – Returned to Japan and was later held by the Soviet Union as a prisoner of war for over a year after the war ended.
  • 1947 – Dismissed from the Japanese Foreign Ministry, possibly due to his defiance during the war; he spent the following decades in relative obscurity.
  • 1969–1985 – Lived quietly while stories of his actions gradually came to light, thanks to the efforts of survivors and their families.
  • 1985 – Honored by Israel’s Yad Vashem as one of the “Righteous Among the Nations” for risking his life and career to save Jews during the Holocaust.
  • 1986 – Passed away on July 31 in Kamakura, Japan; his legacy continues to inspire acts of moral courage and humanitarianism around the world.

Here is a quote from Chiune Sugihara:

"I may have disobeyed my government, but if I hadn't I would have been disobeying God."
